Conjugating a verb means changing its form to match the other grammatical elements that give it meaning. These elements include things like tense (when the verb took place) and person (who carried out the verb).

Helpfully, many Spanish verbs follow one of three fixed conjugation patterns for each tense, with each pattern comprising a specific verb ending for each person. Verbs that follow these patterns are known as regular verbs, and learning the three patterns for each tense will give you access to thousands of verbs!

The pattern of verb endings that we use in each tense depends upon the construction of the verb's infinitive form.

The infinitive form (and the stem)

A verb's infinitive is its basic (unconjugated) form — meaning the verb hasn't been changed to match, say, a tense or person. We often precede a verb's infinitive with “to.”

In Spanish, all infinitives end with either the letters -ar, -er, or -ir. When conjugating a regular verb in a given tense, it is these letters that determine which pattern of verb endings we follow in conveying the relevant person.

The infinitive (or “to”) forms of the verbs we'll use in this guide are:

  • hablar meaning “to talk/speak,”
  • comer meaning “to eat,”
  • and vivir meaning “to live.”

If you take away the final two letters of a Spanish verb's infinitive form, you're left with what is known as the stem. A verb's stem provides its meaning, and generally doesn't change during conjugation (though, as we'll see, it does change in some cases when the vocal stress is placed upon it).

The stems of the verbs we'll use in this guide are:
  • habl- from “hablar,”
  • com- from “comer,”
  • and viv- from “vivir.”

Conjugating regular verbs

When we conjugate a regular Spanish verb in a given tense, we generally do so in one of two ways:

  1. We combine the verb's stem with the relevant verb ending for the person carrying out the verb.
  2. We combine the verb's infinitive with the relevant verb ending for the person carrying out the verb.

The persons we'll refer to in this guide are:

  • yo meaning “I,”
  • tú meaning “you,”
  • él/ella meaning “he/she,”
  • nostotros/nosotras meaning “we (masculine)/we (feminine),”
  • vostotros/vosotras meaning “you (plural; masculine)/you (plural; feminine),”
  • and ellos/ellas meaning “they (masculine)/they (feminine).”

The verb ending patterns

Now that we've covered the basics of conjugation, it's time to learn the verb ending patterns for each tense.

There are loads of great resources out there for learning what the different tenses mean and when to use them. So this section doesn't rehash all that information. Instead, you should treat it as a study guide for memorising the verb ending patterns and referring back to if you get stuck while practising.

The examples given below use the verbs “hablar,” “comer,” and “vivir” to illustrate the process of conjugation. However, once you've learned the patterns, you can apply them to any regular Spanish verb.

In other words, for any given tense:

  • all regular verbs ending in “-ar” (known as “-ar verbs”) follow the pattern of verb endings given for “hablar,”
  • all regular verbs ending in “-er” (“-er verbs”) follow the endings given for “comer,”
  • and all regular verbs ending in “-ir” (“-ir verbs”) follow the endings given for “vivir.”

So by learning the three patterns for each of the following tenses, you'll gain access to thousands of verbs!

  • Present simple
  • Preterite
  • Imperfect
  • Future
  • Conditional
  • Reflexive
  • Present continuous
  • Perfect
  • Pluperfect
  • Present subjunctive
  • Positive imperative
  • Negative imperative

Present simple (stem + ?)

We form the present simple tense by combining the verb's stem with the relevant ending:

  • The verb endings for regular “-ar” verbs are: -o, -as, -a, -amos, -áis, and -an.
  • The verb endings for regular “-er” verbs are: -o, -es, -e, -emos, -éis, and -en.
  • The verb endings for regular “-ir” verbs are: -o, -es, -e, -imos, -ís, and -en.
hablar comer vivir
yo hablo como vivo
tú hablas comes vives
él/ella habla come vive
nos. hablamos comemos vivimos
vos. habláis coméis vivís
ells. hablan comen viven

Preterite (stem + ?)

We form the preterite by combining the verb's stem with the relevant ending:

  • The verb endings for regular “-ar” verbs are: -é, -aste, -ó, -amos, -asteis, and -aron.
  • The verb endings for regular “-er” verbs are: -í, -iste, -ió, -imos, -isteis, and -ieron.
  • The verb endings for regular “-ir” verbs are: -í, -iste, -ió, -imos, -isteis, and -ieron.
hablar comer vivir
yo hablé comí viví
tú hablaste comiste viviste
él/ella habló comió vivió
nos. hablamos comimos vivimos
vos. hablasteis comisteis vivisteis
ells. hablaron comieron vivieron

Imperfect (stem + ?)

We form the imperfect tense by combining the verb's stem with the relevant ending:

  • The verb endings for regular “-ar” verbs are: -aba, -abas, -aba, -ábamos, -abais, and -aban.
  • The verb endings for regular “-er” verbs are: -ía, -ías, -ía, -íamos, -íais, and -ían.
  • The verb endings for regular “-ir” verbs are: -ía, -ías, -ía, -íamos, -íais, and -ían.
hablar comer vivir
yo hablaba comía vivía
tú hablabas comías vivías
él/ella hablaba comía vivía
nos. hablábamos comíamos vivíamos
vos. hablabais comíais vivíais
ells. hablaban comían vivían

Future (infinitive + ?)

We form the future tense by combining the verb's infinitive with the relevant ending:

  • The verb endings for regular “-ar” verbs are: -aré, -arás, -ará, -aremos, -aréis, and -arán.
  • The verb endings for regular “-er” verbs are: -é, -ás, -á, -emos, -éis, and -án.
  • The verb endings for regular “-ir” verbs are: -é, -ás, -á, -emos, -éis, and -án.
hablar comer vivir
yo hablaré comeré viviré
tú hablarás comerás vivirás
él/ella hablará comerá vivirá
nos. hablaremos comeremos viviremos
vos. hablaréis comeréis viviréis
ells. hablarán comerán vivirán

Reflexive (infinitive + ?)

Reflexive verbs work a little differently to other verbs. We form reflexive verbs in the present tense by preceding the verb's present tense form with the relevant reflexive pronoun:

  • The reflexive pronouns for all verbs are: me, te, se, nos, os, and se.
  • The reflexive verbs we'll use as examples are:
    • bañarse meaning “to take a bath,”
    • creerse meaning “to believe,” and
    • dormirse meaning “to go to sleep.”
  • Note that dormirse is an example of a verb whose stem does change when stressed (see the conjugations for yo, tú, él/ella, and ellos/ellas), but not when unstressed (see the conjugations for nosotros/nosotras and vosotros/vosotras).
hablar comer vivir
yo me baño me creo me duermo
tú te bañas te crees te duermes
él/ella se baña se cree se duerme
nos. nos bañamos nos creemos nos dormimos
vos. os bañamos os creéis os dormís
ells. se bañan se creen se duermen
