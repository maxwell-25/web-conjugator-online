Conjugating a verb means changing its form to match the other grammatical elements that give it meaning. These elements include things like tense (when did the verb take place?) and person (who carried out the verb?)

Many Spanish verbs follow one of three fixed conjugation patterns for each tense. Most of these patterns combine either the verb's infinitive form or its stem (terms we'll return to in a moment) with a specific ending for each person.

Verbs that follow these patterns are known as regular verbs, and simply learning the three patterns for each tense will give you access to thousands of verbs!

The infinitive form

A verb's infinitive is its basic (unconjugated) form — meaning the verb isn't changed to match, say, a tense or person. We often precede a verb's infinitive with "to."

In Spanish, all infinitives end with either the letters "-ar," "-er," or "-ir." The infinitive (or "to") forms of the verbs we'll use in this guide are:

  • hablar meaning "to talk/speak,"
  • comer meaning "to eat,"
  • and vivir meaning "to live."

The stem

A verb's stem provides its meaning, and generally doesn't change during conjugation. In Spanish, it can be thought of as comprising a verb's infinitive minus the last two letters ("-ar," "-er," or "-ir").

The stems of the verbs we'll use in this guide are:

  • habl- from "hablar,"
  • com- from "comer,"
  • and viv- from "vivir."

The person

The grammatical category person describes the subject who performs a verb. When conjugating Spanish verbs, we capture this verb-person relationship by combining the imperative or stem with a given verb ending.

The persons we'll use in this guide are:

  • yo meaning "I,"
  • tú meaning "you,"
  • él/ella meaning "he/she,"
  • nostotros/nosotras meaning "we (masculine)/we (feminine),"
  • vostotros/vosotras meaning "you (plural; masculine)/you (plural; feminine),"
  • and ellos/ellas meaning "they (masculine)/they (feminine)."
